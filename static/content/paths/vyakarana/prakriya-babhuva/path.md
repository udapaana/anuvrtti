## concept - The Perfect Tense: Witnessed Past

The @term[liṭ] (perfect) expresses past action NOT witnessed by the speaker — remote past, narrative past, or traditional accounts:

@deva[रामो रावणं जघान] — "Rāma slew Rāvaṇa" (in the story)

The perfect is distinctive for its **reduplication** — the root is doubled in a specific way. This path traces @deva[बभूव] "he was/became" from root @deva[भू].

@prakriya[bhU + liT + Nal]

---

## concept - Step 1: The Input

We start with:

1. **Root**: @deva[भू] "to be, become"
2. **Meaning**: past action (remote/unwitnessed)
3. **Person/Number**: 3rd singular

The perfect tense will require:
- @deva[लिट्] lakāra
- Reduplication of the root
- Special perfect endings

---

## 3.2.115 - Add the Perfect Marker

**Key Terms:** parokṣa, liṭ

@deva[परोक्षे लिट्] — The affix @deva[लिट्] comes when the action is @term[parokṣa] (unwitnessed, beyond sight).

This adds the @deva[लिट्] marker:

> @deva[भू] + @deva[लिट्]

@deva[लिट्] signals "perfect tense." The @deva[ट्] is @term[it] (marker), leaving @deva[लि] as the meaningful part.

---

## 3.4.78 - Select the Perfect Ending

**Key Terms:** Nal, liT

@deva[तिप्तस्झि...] — [The @deva[ल्] is replaced by endings.]

For 3rd singular @term[parasmaipada] in @deva[लिट्], the ending is @deva[णल्]:

> @deva[भू] + @deva[णल्]

@deva[णल्] is specific to the perfect. The @deva[ण्] is @term[it] (triggers @term[vṛddhi] in some contexts), and @deva[ल्] is the final @term[it]. The pronounced ending is... nothing! @deva[णल्] reduces to zero after @term[it]-deletion.

But @deva[णल्] is @term[ṇit], which has important effects.

---

## 6.1.8 - Reduplicate the Root

**Key Terms:** liṭi, dhātu, dvirvacana

@deva[लिटि धातोरनभ्यासस्य] — In @deva[लिट्], a root that hasn't been reduplicated undergoes @term[dvirvacana].

This is the key perfect rule: DOUBLE the root.

> @deva[भू] → @deva[भू भू]

The first @deva[भू] is the **@term[abhyāsa]** (reduplicative syllable). The second is the **@term[abhyasya]** (base). Now rules will modify the @term[abhyāsa].

---

## 7.4.59 - Shorten the Abhyāsa Vowel

**Key Terms:** hrasva, abhyāsa

@deva[ह्रस्वः] — The vowel of the @term[abhyāsa] becomes short.

The @deva[ऊ] in the first @deva[भू] shortens to @deva[उ]:

> @deva[भू भू] → @deva[भु भू]

Long vowels in reduplication are shortened. This gives the characteristic "light-heavy" pattern of perfect forms.

---

## 7.4.60 - Simplify Consonant Clusters

**Key Terms:** abhyāsa, hal

@deva[हलादिः शेषः] — In the @term[abhyāsa], only the initial consonant remains [if there's a cluster].

Here, @deva[भ्] is already a single consonant, so no change. But for roots like @deva[स्था]:

> @deva[स्था स्था] → @deva[त स्था] (cluster @deva[स्थ्] → @deva[त्])

---

## 7.4.62 - Handle Aspirates

**Key Terms:** abhyāsa, cuṭū

@deva[कुहोश्चुः] — Gutturals and @deva[ह्] in the @term[abhyāsa] become palatals.

This doesn't apply to @deva[भ्] (a labial). But for @deva[हन्]:

> @deva[ह ह] → @deva[ज ह] (@deva[ह्] → @deva[ज्] in abhyāsa)

---

## 7.4.63 - Remove Aspiration

**Key Terms:** abhyāsa, jhal

@deva[न अभ्यासे] — In the @term[abhyāsa], there's no aspiration [of stops other than @deva[ह्]].

@deva[भ्] is an aspirated labial. In the @term[abhyāsa], it loses aspiration:

> @deva[भु भू] → @deva[बु भू]

The @deva[भ्] → @deva[ब्]. Now we have:

> @deva[ब] + @deva[भू] + ending

---

## 7.2.115 - Vṛddhi Before Ṇit Affixes

**Key Terms:** ṇit, vṛddhi, aṅga

@deva[अचो ञ्णिति] — The vowel of the @term[aṅga] takes @term[vṛddhi] before @term[ñit] or @term[ṇit] affixes.

@deva[णल्] is @term[ṇit]. So the root vowel @deva[ऊ] takes @term[vṛddhi]:

> @deva[ऊ] → @deva[औ]...

But wait — @deva[ऊ] is a labial vowel, and its @term[vṛddhi] is @deva[औ]. However, @deva[भू] is special.

Actually, for @deva[भू] in the perfect, a different pattern applies. Let me correct:

The @deva[णल्] ending here doesn't trigger @term[vṛddhi] of @deva[ऊ] in the standard way. The @deva[ऊ] remains @deva[ऊ], giving:

> @deva[ब] + @deva[भू] + @deva[∅] (णल् = zero)

---

## 6.1.77 - Vowel Sandhi

**Key Terms:** ik, yaṇ, aci

@deva[इको यणचि] — An @pratyahara[ik] vowel becomes @pratyahara[yaṇ] when followed by a vowel.

The @deva[उ] of @deva[बु] is @pratyahara[ik]. If followed by a vowel, it would become @deva[व्]. But here @deva[भ्] follows (a consonant), so this doesn't apply directly.

The @term[abhyāsa] @deva[ब] + @term[abhyasya] @deva[भू] combine as:

> @deva[बबू] → with aspiration loss in abhyāsa → @deva[बभू]

Wait, let me re-trace. After @ref[7.4.63]:
- @term[abhyāsa]: @deva[ब] (from @deva[भ्] + short @deva[उ])
- @term[abhyasya]: @deva[भू]

Combined: @deva[बभू]

---

## 3.4.82 - Zero Ending

**Key Terms:** Nal, parassaippada

After @term[it]-deletion, @deva[णल्] becomes zero (no overt ending):

> @deva[बभू] + @deva[∅] → @deva[बभू]

But we need the final form @deva[बभूव]. Where does the @deva[व] come from?

---

## 6.4.88 - Insert व Before Certain Endings

**Key Terms:** bhū, liṭi

@deva[भुवो वुग्लुङ्लिटोः] — Before @deva[लुङ्] and @deva[लिट्] endings, @deva[भू] takes the augment @deva[वुक्].

The @deva[वुक्] (= @deva[व्]) is inserted after @deva[भू]:

> @deva[बभू] + @deva[व्] → @deva[बभूव]

This is a special rule for root @deva[भू] in these tenses.

## concept - The Complete Rule Chain

| # | Rule | Operation |
|---|------|-----------|
| 1 | @ref[3.2.115] | Add @deva[लिट्] (perfect marker) |
| 2 | @ref[3.4.78] | Select @deva[णल्] (3sg perfect ending) |
| 3 | @ref[6.1.8] | Reduplicate: @deva[भू] → @deva[भू भू] |
| 4 | @ref[7.4.59] | Shorten abhyāsa: @deva[भू भू] → @deva[भु भू] |
| 5 | @ref[7.4.63] | De-aspirate: @deva[भु] → @deva[बु] |
| 6 | @ref[6.4.88] | Insert @deva[व्]: @deva[बभू] → @deva[बभूव] |
| 7 | @ref[1.3.9] | Delete @term[it] markers |

Final form: **@deva[बभूव]** — "he was, he became"

## concept - The Reduplication Pattern

The @term[abhyāsa] transformations follow a consistent pattern:

| Root | Initial Reduplication | After Shortening | After De-aspiration | Perfect 3sg |
|------|----------------------|------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| @deva[भू] | @deva[भू भू] | @deva[भु भू] | @deva[बु भू] | @deva[बभूव] |
| @deva[कृ] | @deva[कृ कृ] | @deva[कृ कृ] (already short) | — | @deva[चकार] |
| @deva[गम्] | @deva[गम् गम्] | @deva[ग गम्] | — | @deva[जगाम] |
| @deva[पठ्] | @deva[पठ् पठ्] | @deva[प पठ्] | — | @deva[पपाठ] |

@deva[गम्] → @deva[जगाम] "he went" (guttural → palatal; vṛddhi of root vowel)

@deva[पत्] → @deva[पपात] "he fell"

## concept - Why Reduplication?

Reduplication in the perfect is an ancient Indo-European feature, preserved in Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, and other languages:

| Language | "I have done" |
|----------|---------------|
| Sanskrit | @deva[चकार] |
| Greek | πέπραγα (pepraga) |
| Latin | fēcī (from *fe-fak-) |

The reduplicative syllable originally marked completed aspect. Pāṇini's rules precisely encode this inherited pattern.
