## concept - What is a @term[prātipadika]?

In English, the word "king" can stand alone in a sentence: "The king rules." In Sanskrit, it cannot. The stem @deva[राज] ("king") is not a usable word by itself — it needs a @term[vibhakti] to tell you its role in the sentence:

| Form           | @term[vibhakti]                  | Meaning              |
| -------------- | -------------------------------- | -------------------- |
| @deva[राजा]    | @term[prathamā] @term[ekavacana] | "the king" (subject) |
| @deva[राजानम्] | @term[dvitīyā] @term[ekavacana]  | "the king" (object)  |
| @deva[राज्ञा]  | @term[tṛtīyā] @term[ekavacana]   | "by the king"        |
| @deva[राज्ञः]  | @term[ṣaṣṭhī] @term[ekavacana]   | "of the king"        |

The bare form — the part that carries the meaning before any ending is attached — is called @term[prātipadika] (nominal stem).

Think of it this way: the stem is like a root vegetable still in the ground. You can't eat it yet. You need to pull it up and prepare it — that preparation is the @term[sup] ending. Once the ending is attached, you have a @term[pada], a complete @term[subanta].

**Why does this matter?** Everything in the noun system flows from here. Before you can decline @deva[देव] into @deva[देवः], @deva[देवम्], @deva[देवेन], you must first understand what @deva[देव] is and why it qualifies as a stem.

---

## concept - Three Sources of Stems

Where do nominal stems come from? Panini identifies three derived sources, plus a large class of basic, underived stems.

**1. @deva[कृत्] — Stems from Verbal Roots**

Add a primary suffix to a root, and you get a noun:

| Root                 | Suffix        | Stem         | Meaning         |
| -------------------- | ------------- | ------------ | --------------- |
| @deva[कृ] "to do"    | @deva[तृच्]   | @deva[कर्तृ] | "doer, agent"   |
| @deva[पच्] "to cook" | @deva[तृच्]   | @deva[पक्तृ] | "cook"          |
| @deva[गम्] "to go"   | @deva[क्तिन्] | @deva[गति]   | "going, motion" |
| @deva[भू] "to be"    | @deva[क्तिन्] | @deva[भूति]  | "prosperity"    |

**2. @deva[तद्धित] — Stems from Other Nouns**

Add a secondary suffix to an existing noun, and you derive a new noun:

| Base noun                 | Suffix       | Stem             | Meaning                 |
| ------------------------- | ------------ | ---------------- | ----------------------- |
| @deva[राम]                | @deva[अण्]   | @deva[रामायण]    | "relating to Rama"      |
| @deva[विद्या] "knowledge" | @deva[मतुप्] | @deva[विद्यावत्] | "one who has knowledge" |
| @deva[पुत्र] "son"        | @deva[ठक्]   | @deva[पौत्रिक]   | "relating to a son"     |

**3. @deva[समास] — Compound Stems**

Combine two or more words into one stem:

| Components                  | Compound        | Meaning      |
| --------------------------- | --------------- | ------------ |
| @deva[राजन्] + @deva[पुरुष] | @deva[राजपुरुष] | "king's man" |
| @deva[नील] + @deva[उत्पल]   | @deva[नीलोत्पल] | "blue lotus" |
| @deva[महत्] + @deva[आत्मन्] | @deva[महात्मन्] | "great soul" |

**4. Basic (Underived) Stems**

Many stems are simply... words. They don't come from roots or other nouns. They just exist in the language:

@deva[देव] "god," @deva[वन] "forest," @deva[कन्या] "girl," @deva[अग्नि] "fire," @deva[गुरु] "teacher," @deva[नदी] "river," @deva[मधु] "honey"

All four types are @deva[प्रातिपदिक]. Panini defines this in the next two sutras.

## 1.2.45 - अर्थवदधातुरप्रत्ययः प्रातिपदिकम्

**Key Terms:** @deva[अर्थवत्], @deva[अधातु], @deva[अप्रत्यय], @deva[प्रातिपदिक]

A meaningful form (@deva[अर्थवत्]) that is neither a verbal root (@deva[अधातु]) nor an affix (@deva[अप्रत्यय]) is called @deva[प्रातिपदिक].

This is the formal definition. It has three conditions — all three must be met:

| Condition       | Meaning      | Why it's needed                                                                            |
| --------------- | ------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| @deva[अर्थवत्]  | Has meaning  | Excludes meaningless sound sequences                                                       |
| @deva[अधातु]    | Not a root   | Excludes @deva[भू], @deva[पच्], @deva[गम्] etc. — roots get verb endings, not case endings |
| @deva[अप्रत्यय] | Not an affix | Excludes @deva[ति], @deva[सु], @deva[क्त्वा] etc. — affixes are parts, not stems           |

**Examples of @deva[प्रातिपदिक]:**

| Stem         | Meaning | Why it qualifies                     |
| ------------ | ------- | ------------------------------------ |
| @deva[देव]   | god     | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[राजन्] | king    | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[वन]    | forest  | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[कन्या] | girl    | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[अग्नि] | fire    | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[गुरु]  | teacher | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |
| @deva[पितृ]  | father  | Meaningful, not a root, not an affix |

**What does NOT qualify:**

| Form          | Why not                          |
| ------------- | -------------------------------- |
| @deva[भू]     | It's a @deva[धातु] (verbal root) |
| @deva[ति]     | It's a @deva[प्रत्यय] (affix)    |
| @deva[क्त्वा] | It's a @deva[प्रत्यय] (affix)    |
| random noise  | Not @deva[अर्थवत्] (no meaning)  |

This sutra covers the basic, underived stems. But what about stems formed by adding @deva[कृत्] suffixes, @deva[तद्धित] suffixes, or compounding? The next sutra addresses those.

## 1.2.46 - कृत्तद्धितसमासाश्च

**Key Terms:** @deva[कृत्], @deva[तद्धित], @deva[समास], @deva[प्रातिपदिक]

Forms ending in @deva[कृत्] affixes, @deva[तद्धित] affixes, or compounds (@deva[समास]) are also called @deva[प्रातिपदिक].

The word @deva[च] ("and, also") tells us: these are **in addition** to the basic stems defined in @ref[1.2.45]. Even though a @deva[कृत्] form technically _contains_ an affix, the whole derived unit is now a stem — ready to receive case endings.

**@deva[कृत्] examples — nouns derived from roots:**

| Root                   | + Suffix      | = Stem        | Meaning           |
| ---------------------- | ------------- | ------------- | ----------------- |
| @deva[कृ] "to do"      | @deva[तृच्]   | @deva[कर्तृ]  | "doer"            |
| @deva[पठ्] "to read"   | @deva[तृच्]   | @deva[पठितृ]  | "reader"          |
| @deva[नी] "to lead"    | @deva[क्तिन्] | @deva[नीति]   | "policy, conduct" |
| @deva[स्था] "to stand" | @deva[क्तिन्] | @deva[स्थिति] | "state, position" |

**@deva[तद्धित] examples — nouns derived from other nouns:**

| Base               | + Suffix     | = Stem        | Meaning              |
| ------------------ | ------------ | ------------- | -------------------- |
| @deva[राम]         | @deva[अण्]   | @deva[रामायण] | "relating to Rama"   |
| @deva[दक्ष]        | @deva[अण्]   | @deva[दाक्ष]  | "relating to Daksha" |
| @deva[धन] "wealth" | @deva[मतुप्] | @deva[धनवत्]  | "wealthy"            |

**@deva[समास] examples — compound stems:**

| Components                  | = Stem           | Meaning            |
| --------------------------- | ---------------- | ------------------ |
| @deva[राजन्] + @deva[पुरुष] | @deva[राजपुरुष]  | "king's man"       |
| @deva[पीत] + @deva[अम्बर]   | @deva[पीताम्बर]  | "yellow-robed"     |
| @deva[बहु] + @deva[व्रीहि]  | @deva[बहुव्रीहि] | "having much rice" |

All of these — @deva[कर्तृ], @deva[रामायण], @deva[राजपुरुष] — are @deva[प्रातिपदिक]. They can now receive case endings like @deva[सु], @deva[अम्], @deva[टा] to become complete words.

## concept - Case Endings Overview

A stem becomes a word by receiving a case ending. Sanskrit has **seven cases** (plus a vocative), each expressing a different relationship between the noun and the action in the sentence.

Let's see all seven cases in one sentence about @deva[राम]:

| Case               | Sanskrit Name   | Ending (sg.) | Example               | Meaning                                             |
| ------------------ | --------------- | ------------ | --------------------- | --------------------------------------------------- |
| 1st (Nominative)   | @deva[प्रथमा]   | @deva[सु]    | @deva[रामः गच्छति]    | "**Rama** goes" (subject)                           |
| 2nd (Accusative)   | @deva[द्वितीया] | @deva[अम्]   | @deva[रामं पश्यति]    | "He sees **Rama**" (object)                         |
| 3rd (Instrumental) | @deva[तृतीया]   | @deva[टा]    | @deva[रामेण सह]       | "together **with Rama**" (instrument/accompaniment) |
| 4th (Dative)       | @deva[चतुर्थी]  | @deva[ङे]    | @deva[रामाय ददाति]    | "He gives **to Rama**" (recipient)                  |
| 5th (Ablative)     | @deva[पञ्चमी]   | @deva[ङसि]   | @deva[रामात् आगच्छति] | "He comes **from Rama**" (source)                   |
| 6th (Genitive)     | @deva[षष्ठी]    | @deva[ङस्]   | @deva[रामस्य पुत्रः]  | "**Rama's** son" (possession)                       |
| 7th (Locative)     | @deva[सप्तमी]   | @deva[ङि]    | @deva[रामे विश्वासः]  | "Trust **in Rama**" (location)                      |

Each case has three numbers: singular (@deva[एकवचन]), dual (@deva[द्विवचन]), and plural (@deva[बहुवचन]). That gives us 7 x 3 = **21 endings** total.

These 21 endings are collectively called @deva[सुप्] — named after the first ending @deva[सु] and the last ending @deva[सुप्] in the list. The next sutra gives us all 21.

## 4.1.2 - स्वौजसमौट्छष्टाभ्याम्भिस्ङेभ्याम्भ्यस्ङसिभ्याम्भ्यस्ङसोसाम्ङ्योस्सुप्

**Key Terms:** @deva[सुप्], @deva[विभक्ति], @deva[प्रत्यय]

This single sutra lists all 21 case endings for nouns. They are read out as a continuous string, then parsed into a 7 x 3 table:

| Case                | Singular   | Dual          | Plural       |
| ------------------- | ---------- | ------------- | ------------ |
| 1st @deva[प्रथमा]   | @deva[सु]  | @deva[औ]      | @deva[जस्]   |
| 2nd @deva[द्वितीया] | @deva[अम्] | @deva[औट्]    | @deva[शस्]   |
| 3rd @deva[तृतीया]   | @deva[टा]  | @deva[भ्याम्] | @deva[भिस्]  |
| 4th @deva[चतुर्थी]  | @deva[ङे]  | @deva[भ्याम्] | @deva[भ्यस्] |
| 5th @deva[पञ्चमी]   | @deva[ङसि] | @deva[भ्याम्] | @deva[भ्यस्] |
| 6th @deva[षष्ठी]    | @deva[ङस्] | @deva[ओस्]    | @deva[आम्]   |
| 7th @deva[सप्तमी]   | @deva[ङि]  | @deva[ओस्]    | @deva[सुप्]  |

Notice some endings are shared: @deva[भ्याम्] serves the 3rd, 4th, and 5th dual; @deva[भ्यस्] serves the 4th and 5th plural; @deva[ओस्] serves the 6th and 7th dual.

**Marker letters in these endings:**

Many of these endings contain @deva[इत्] letters (markers) that are deleted in the final form but serve grammatical purposes:

| Ending     | Marker    | Real form  | Purpose of marker                            |
| ---------- | --------- | ---------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| @deva[जस्] | @deva[ज्] | @deva[अस्] | @deva[ज्] triggers special neuter rules      |
| @deva[औट्] | @deva[ट्] | @deva[औ]   | @deva[ट्] prevents certain sandhi operations |
| @deva[शस्] | @deva[श्] | @deva[अस्] | @deva[श्] triggers special rules             |
| @deva[टा]  | @deva[ट्] | @deva[आ]   | @deva[ट्] marks this as a replacement target |

These 21 endings attach to stems after @deva[ङी], @deva[आप्], or directly after the @deva[प्रातिपदिक] — as the previous sutra @ref[4.1.1] tells us.

## 4.1.1 - ङ्याप्प्रातिपदिकात्

**Key Terms:** @deva[ङी], @deva[आप्], @deva[प्रातिपदिक]

The @deva[सुप्] endings (and the operations taught from this point forward) come after @deva[ङी], @deva[आप्], or directly after a @deva[प्रातिपदिक].

This is an @deva[अधिकार] (governing rule). It sets the stage for the entire noun-formation section of the Ashtadhyayi. Everything that follows — all 21 case endings, all the sound changes — applies to forms built on a @deva[प्रातिपदिक].

**@deva[ङी] and @deva[आप्] — Feminine Stem Formation**

Before case endings are added, some stems first receive a feminine suffix:

| Masculine Stem      | + Feminine Suffix      | = Feminine Stem              | Then + Case Ending                          |
| ------------------- | ---------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| @deva[देव] "god"    | @deva[ङीप्] → @deva[ई] | @deva[देवी] "goddess"        | @deva[देवी] + @deva[सु] = @deva[देवी]       |
| @deva[बाल] "boy"    | @deva[टाप्] → @deva[आ] | @deva[बाला] "girl"           | @deva[बाला] + @deva[सु] = @deva[बाला]       |
| @deva[कर्तृ] "doer" | @deva[ङीप्] → @deva[ई] | @deva[कर्त्री] "female doer" | @deva[कर्त्री] + @deva[सु] = @deva[कर्त्री] |

The @deva[आप्] group includes @deva[टाप्], @deva[डाप्], and @deva[चाप्] — three feminine suffixes that all reduce to @deva[आ] after their marker letters are deleted.

**The process for building a noun:**

@deva[प्रातिपदिक] → (optionally add @deva[ङी]/@deva[आप्]) → add @deva[सुप्] ending → apply sound changes → @deva[पद]

## 1.4.14 - सुप्तिङन्तं पदम्

**Key Terms:** @deva[सुप्], @deva[तिङ्], @deva[पद]

That which ends in @deva[सुप्] (a case ending) or @deva[तिङ्] (a verb ending) is called @deva[पद].

This is one of the most important definitions in the entire grammar. A @deva[पद] is a **complete word** — something that can actually appear in a sentence. Without an ending, a stem or root is just raw material.

**Two types of @deva[पद]:**

| Type                    | Ending set                    | Count                          | Example                                 |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------- | ------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| @deva[सुबन्त] (nominal) | @deva[सुप्] — 21 case endings | 7 cases x 3 numbers            | @deva[देवः], @deva[देवम्], @deva[देवेन] |
| @deva[तिङन्त] (verbal)  | @deva[तिङ्] — 18 verb endings | 3 persons x 3 numbers x 2 sets | @deva[पचति], @deva[पचतः], @deva[पचन्ति] |

**Examples showing stem → @deva[पद]:**

| Stem/Root       | + Ending            | = @deva[पद]       | Meaning         |
| --------------- | ------------------- | ----------------- | --------------- |
| @deva[ब्राह्मण] | + @deva[सु] (N.sg)  | @deva[ब्राह्मणः]  | "the Brahmana"  |
| @deva[ब्राह्मण] | + @deva[जस्] (N.pl) | @deva[ब्राह्मणाः] | "the Brahmanas" |
| @deva[वन]       | + @deva[ङि] (L.sg)  | @deva[वने]        | "in the forest" |
| @deva[भू]       | + @deva[तिप्] (3sg) | @deva[भवति]       | "he/she/it is"  |

**Why this matters:** Many rules in the Ashtadhyayi apply only to @deva[पद] — complete words. For instance, certain sandhi rules operate at word boundaries, which means they apply only between @deva[पद]s. If something hasn't received its @deva[सुप्] or @deva[तिङ्] ending yet, those rules don't kick in.

## 1.4.103 - सुपः

**Key Terms:** @deva[सुप्], @deva[विभक्ति]

The @deva[सुप्] endings are called @deva[विभक्ति].

The term @deva[विभक्ति] literally means "division, distinction" — the case endings **distinguish** the roles nouns play in a sentence. Is @deva[राम] the doer or the object? The @deva[विभक्ति] tells you:

| @deva[विभक्ति]      | Technical name | What it distinguishes    |
| ------------------- | -------------- | ------------------------ |
| 1st @deva[प्रथमा]   | Nominative     | The subject or predicate |
| 2nd @deva[द्वितीया] | Accusative     | The object of action     |
| 3rd @deva[तृतीया]   | Instrumental   | The instrument or agent  |
| 4th @deva[चतुर्थी]  | Dative         | The recipient            |
| 5th @deva[पञ्चमी]   | Ablative       | The source or cause      |
| 6th @deva[षष्ठी]    | Genitive       | Possession or relation   |
| 7th @deva[सप्तमी]   | Locative       | The location or domain   |

**Why a technical name?** Many later rules say "when @deva[विभक्ति] is present" or "apply this after @deva[विभक्ति]." Having the term @deva[विभक्ति] lets Panini refer to all 21 @deva[सुप्] endings as a group in a single word.

Note: @deva[तिङ्] endings for verbs are also called @deva[विभक्ति] by @ref[1.4.104]. So @deva[विभक्ति] covers both noun endings and verb endings — but in the context of nouns, it always means the @deva[सुप्] endings.

## concept - Stem Classification by Final Sound

The sound a stem ends in determines which sound changes occur when case endings are attached. This is why Sanskrit grammars organize declension patterns by the stem's final sound.

**Vowel-ending stems:**

| Final sound | Examples                                 | Gender(s)          | Declension name |
| ----------- | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------ | --------------- |
| @deva[अ]    | @deva[देव], @deva[वन], @deva[बाल]        | masc., neut.       | @deva[अ]-stems  |
| @deva[आ]    | @deva[कन्या], @deva[बाला], @deva[विद्या] | fem.               | @deva[आ]-stems  |
| @deva[इ]    | @deva[अग्नि], @deva[कवि], @deva[मति]     | masc., fem.        | @deva[इ]-stems  |
| @deva[ई]    | @deva[नदी], @deva[देवी], @deva[लक्ष्मी]  | fem.               | @deva[ई]-stems  |
| @deva[उ]    | @deva[गुरु], @deva[शिशु], @deva[मधु]     | masc., fem., neut. | @deva[उ]-stems  |
| @deva[ऊ]    | @deva[भू], @deva[वधू], @deva[चमू]        | fem.               | @deva[ऊ]-stems  |
| @deva[ऋ]    | @deva[पितृ], @deva[कर्तृ], @deva[मातृ]   | masc., fem.        | @deva[ऋ]-stems  |

**Consonant-ending stems:**

| Final sound | Examples                    | Notes                                      |
| ----------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| @deva[न्]   | @deva[राजन्], @deva[आत्मन्] | Very common                                |
| @deva[स्]   | @deva[मनस्], @deva[तपस्]    | Neuter @deva[अस्]-stems                    |
| @deva[त्]   | @deva[मरुत्], @deva[भगवत्]  | Includes @deva[वत्]/@deva[मत्] possessives |
| @deva[द्]   | @deva[विपद्], @deva[सम्पद्] | Fewer in number                            |
| @deva[श्]   | @deva[दिश्], @deva[विश्]    | Direction words                            |

**Why this classification matters:**

When @deva[सुप्] endings are added, different sound changes apply depending on the stem's final sound. For example, @deva[देव] (ending in @deva[अ]) + @deva[भिस्] becomes @deva[देवैः] (the @deva[भिस्] is replaced by @deva[ऐस्] after @deva[अ]). But @deva[अग्नि] (ending in @deva[इ]) + @deva[भिस्] becomes @deva[अग्निभिः] (no replacement).

Each stem class has its own declension path in this app. Master the @deva[अ]-stems first — they're by far the most common.

| Three sources     | @deva[कृत्], @deva[तद्धित], @deva[समास] (plus basic underived stems) |
| @deva[सुप्]       | The 21 case endings (7 cases x 3 numbers)                            |
| @deva[विभक्ति]    | The technical name for case endings                                  |
